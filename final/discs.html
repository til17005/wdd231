Discs

Generally there are three types of discs used in disc golf: drivers, mid-range discs, and putters. Drivers are used for long distance throws, mid-range discs are used for shorter throws, and putters are used for short, accurate throws. Each type of disc has its own unique characteristics and flight patterns, which can be used to your advantage on the course.

There are many different brands and models of discs available, each with its own unique characteristics. Some of the most popular brands include Innova, Discraft, and Dynamic Discs.

When choosing a disc, it is important to consider your skill level and the type of throw you are making. For example, if you are a beginner, you may want to start with a putter or a mid-range disc, as these are generally easier to throw and control. As you become more experienced, you can start to experiment with different types of discs and throws.

Disc stability and flight guide

Understanding disc stability is crucial for making accurate throws. Discs are generally classified as overstable, stable, or understable, depending on their flight characteristics.

Overstable discs tend to fade to the left (for right-handed backhand throws) and are great for hyzer throws. Stable discs fly straight and are versatile for various throws, while understable discs turn to the right (for right-handed backhand throws) and are ideal for anhyzer throws.

Additionally, the flight path of a disc is influenced by its speed, glide, turn, and fade ratings. The speed rating indicates how fast the disc can travel, glide refers to how long it stays in the air, turn indicates how much the disc will turn to the right during its flight, and fade indicates how much the disc will fade to the left at the end of its flight.

Understanding these ratings and how they affect the flight of a disc can help you make more accurate throws and choose the right disc for each shot.

Disc Stability Quick Guide

Assuming a right handed backhand throw:

1. Flight Ratings

Focus on the Turn and Fade numbers:

  • Turn: Negative = more understable, 0 or positive = more overstable
  • Fade: Higher = more overstable finish, Lower = straighter finish

2. Stability Types

Type Turn Fade Flight Pattern (RHBH)
Overstable 0 to +1 3 to 5 Hooks left hard at the end
Stable -1 to 0 1 to 2 Straight with slight fade
Understable -2 to -5 0 to 2 Turns right and stays right

3. Field Test

Throw the disc flat with moderate power:

  • Turns right and doesn’t come back → Understable
  • Flies straight and finishes slightly left → Stable
  • Resists turning and finishes hard left → Overstable

4. Turn + Fade = Stability Score

Adding Turn and Fade gives a quick estimate of stability:

Turn + Fade Interpretation
0 Stable – Flies straight with a gentle finish
Positive Overstable – Resists turning, finishes left (RHBH)
Negative Understable – Turns right and may not fade back (RHBH)

Examples:

  • Turn -2, Fade 1 → Score = -1 → Slightly Understable
  • Turn 0, Fade 3 → Score = +3 → Overstable
  • Turn -1, Fade 1 → Score = 0 → Stable

5. Bonus Tips

  • Arm speed matters: Slower arms make discs feel more overstable.
  • Wind effects: Headwinds make discs act more understable.
  • Plastic and wear: Beat-in discs become more understable over time.
